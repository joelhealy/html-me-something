What are Goodeids?

Goodeids are a family of fish (Goodeidae) consisting of approximately 40 species. The vast majority of goodeids are members of a sub-family named Goodeinae. These fish are livebearers and are endemic to Central Mexico. There are a few species in a second sub-family named Empetrichthynae that are egg-layers and are endemic to the Southwest United States. The current theory assumes that these fish all descended from a common ancestor and that the two sub-families were isolated by volcanic and seismic activity over a million years ago and developed separately.

I started keeping fish relatively late in life. My sons received a 10 gallon fish tank for a Christmas present around the year 2000. We went to a local pet store and bought a couple black mollies and a goldfish. My sons quickly lost interest, but when the black mollies had babies I became interested in livebearing fish and have been keeping them ever since. I started looking at AquaBid, a fishy version of eBay. In 2001, I bought my first livebearing Goodied and I have had a special affinity for these Mexican fish to the present day.

Because the egg-laying species of Goodieds from the United States are all protected and illegal to possess, I have concentrated on the livebearing Goodeids from Mexico. I have kept around 30 of the 35 or so species at one time or another. In October of 2011, I flew to France to attend an international symposium on Goodeids. At my peak, I had close to 100 tanks in my basement, but I am currently down to a meager 25 or so. I have never really figured out why I am so interested in these ugly grey fish. It saddens me that so many of the Goodeids have either become extinct in the wild or are well on their way. I wish there was something I could do to protect their habitat for posterity, but I don't think there is.

Allodontichthys

Allodontichthys are one of the torpedo-shaped Goodeids. There are two species of Allodontichthys that I have never kept, Allodontichthys polylepis and Allodontichthys zonistius. The A. polylepis are believed to be extinct in the wild. I maintained A. tamazulae only briefly and never got them to breed for me. The A. hubbsi (named after famous ichthyologist Carl Hubbs) was more prolific and easy to keep.

  • Allodontichthys hubbsi
  • Allodontichthys tamazulae

Alloophorus

Alloophorus robustus (a.k.a The Bulldog Goodeied) is one of the largest goodeids. It has been known to obtain a maximum length of 14 cm. I have only kept this fish once and had no luck breeding my single pair of fish. I bought my stock from a breeder in England.

  • Alloophorus robustus

Allotoca

Allotoca is one of my favorite genera of Goodeids. As a matter of fact, at one time I bought the domain name allotocas.us and tried to build a static website devoted to these fish. There are only two species of Allotca that I have not kept, Allotoca diazi and Allotoca regalis. In fact, I have never even seen A. regalis.

I have had the most luck with A. goslinei and A. zacapuensis. I kept these fish for many, many years and was able to distribute hundreds of these fish to interested people at American Livebearers Association (ALA) conventions over the years. A. goslinei have been extinct in the wild for many years and are only maintained in captivity by a handful of hobbyists.

  • Allotoca catarinae
  • Allotoca dugesii
  • Allotoca goslinei
  • Allotoca maculata
  • Allotoca meeki
  • Allotoca zacapuensis

Ameca

Ameca splendens, the Butterfly Goodeid, are very hardy fish and are easy to maintain in an aquarium. They consume more vegetable content than most of the other Goodieds and are known to eat the algae off the sides of their tanks. They were thought to be extinct in the wild at one point, but due to reintroduction efforts are once again found living in the central plateau of Mexico.

  • Ameca splendens

Ataeniobius

Ataeniobius toweri are another of the torpedo-shaped Goodeids. Their claim to fame is that they are the only Goodeid which does not appear to have trophotaenia, placental-like structures through which females provide nutrition to the fry while they are insider her body. The genus is actually named after this feature of the fish, however more recent studies of the species have reported finding trophotaenia on these fish.

  • Ataeniobius toweri

Chapalichthys

Chapalicthys are a deep-bodied goodeid. Not many hobbyists show a lot of interest in these fish. Outside of a yellow band on the caudal, they are the epitome of ugly grey fish (not to be confused with ugly tan fish or ugly brown fish). Some people think there is a third species called Chapalicthys peraticus, but it is not considered separate in most of the literature.

  • Chapalichthys encaustus
  • Chapalichthys pardalis

Characodon

Characodon is the genus of goodeids that typical hobbyists are most interested in. I believe this is based entirely on their appearance. They are definitely the most colorful of the goodeids. While many of the species designations among the goodeids seem to be based on accidents of history, none is more questionable than Characodon. For example, one of the three species in the genus, Characodon garmani, is based on a single preserved specimen of questionable origin. This species hasn't been seen in nature for a hundred years.

Many populations of Characodon have gone extinct before they could be assigned to one of the other two species. Studies have been conducted recently by different researchers but no consensus has formed yet. One of the definitive descriptions of the genus was written by a Mexican civil engineer named Juan Miguel Artigas Azas.

  • Characodon audax
  • Characodon lateralis

Girardinichthys

This genus of goodeids is relatively rare and difficult to maintain in captivity. They require clean cool water to survive which is little counter-intuitive as one of the only remaining locations where some of the fish can be found is a filthy city park in Mexico City with horrendous water quality due to massive pollution.

  • Girardinichthys multiradiatus
  • Girardinichthys viviparus

Goodea

Goodea are one of the larger goodeids. They are very hardy fish and can be found in large schools in some Mexican lakes. They are one of the few fish that are actually consumed as food in many small villages. They are one of the least endangered of all the goodeids.

  • Goodea atripinnis
  • Goodea gracilis

Ilyodon

Ilyodon are another of the torpedo-shaped goodieds that are very common and in no immediate need of protection. One species has the common name of Basslet Goodeid. This same species is very hardy and easy to maintain in aquaria. In fact, they are so used to poor water quality that some hobbyists make a mistake by changing their water too often.

  • Ilyodon furcidens
  • Ilyodon whitei

Skiffia

Skiffia run the gamut from very common to extinct in the wild. There are huge disagreements as to how to assign recently discovered populations to the existing species. Taxonomists vehemently argue about how to classify these fish. It is very similar to the situation with Characodon. There are "splitters" and "lumpers" and combination in between.

  • Skiffia bilineata
  • Skiffia francesae
  • Skiffia lermae
  • Skiffia multipunctata

Xenoophorus

Although there is currently only one species of Xenoophorus, in the recent past there were three. Like the Characodon, many populations disappeared before they could be properly studied and classified. Juan Miguel Artigas Azas wrote a disheartening article about a distinct population in Illescas which could not survive the continuous pollution and dessication of its natural environment.

  • Xenoophorus captivus

Xenotaenia

There is only one species in the genus Xenotaenia. It is a very hardy fish despite being relatively rare among hobbyists. Some of this is due to its plain appearance. Not many people are anxious to keep these ugly brown fish.

  • Xenotaenia resolanae

Xenotoca

Xenotoca is another goodeid genus that is in flux. It has been assumed for many years that a major reorganization would someday occur. Some of these changes have recently been published in the literature, but there are several more changes to come.

While Xenotoca eiseni is the most common, hardy and prolific of the goodieds, Xenotoca melanosoma is relatively rare and difficult to breed.

  • Xenotoca eiseni
  • Xenotoca melanosoma
  • Xenotoca variata

Zoogoneticus

Zoogoneticus tequila is extinct in nature, although there are recent reports of a successful reintroduction into an artificial outdoor environment. The two other related species are somewhat common in the wild although they are rare in the hobby.

  • Zoogoneticus purhepechus
  • Zoogoneticus quitzeoensis
  • Zoogoneticus tequila
